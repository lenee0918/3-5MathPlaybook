How to Use This Playbook

Start with the planning bar. Use the quarter, assessment type, and big idea to place the standard in the year and clarify the main mathematical goal.

Use “Standard appears in” to see the arc. Some standards are introduced in one unit and strengthened in later units. Use that section to plan review, intervention, and reteaching.

Plan from the CRA/CPA progression. Move from concrete materials to drawings and then to abstract notation. Use the teacher moves to tighten questioning and modeling.

Differentiate with purpose. Use the small-group section to decide whether students need more concrete support, more practice connecting representations, or extension into explanation and application.

Use the EOG-style question as a planning check. It helps clarify the kind of reasoning students should be ready to demonstrate after instruction.

Use the printable visual summary for quick reference. It can support planning conversations, intervention groups, and classroom modeling.

Notice the practice box inside each standard. It names the most relevant mathematical practices for that standard so planning stays intentional without repeating all eight practices every time.

How Mathematical Practices Are Used in This Playbook

The eight Standards for Mathematical Practice are part of North Carolina’s K–8 Mathematics Standard Course of Study. In this playbook, the practices are not listed as a separate checklist under every standard. Instead, each standard card highlights only the most instructionally relevant practices so teachers can see where they naturally belong in planning, modeling, discourse, and problem solving.

MP2 Reason abstractly and quantitatively
MP3 Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others
MP4 Model with mathematics
MP5 Use appropriate tools strategically
MP6 Attend to precision
MP7 Look for and make use of structure
MP8 Look for and express regularity in repeated reasoning

NC.3.OA.1
Interpret multiplication through equal groups and arrays
Quarter(s) Q1–Q2
Assessment type Unit 1 and Unit 4 assessments
Big idea Multiplication grows out of equal groups, repeated addition, arrays, and story situations.

What Students Should Know, Do, and Understand

Know
  • equal groups, arrays, factors, and product
  • multiplication situations can be shown with objects, drawings, and equations
Do
  • represent multiplication with equal groups and arrays
  • explain what each factor and the product mean in context
Understand
  • multiplication describes equal-group situations, not just repeated addition
  • the structure of the model should match the structure of the story

Key Considerations from the NC Unpacking Document

Use these teacher-facing notes to preserve the most important ideas from the unpacking while planning instruction, intervention, and questions.

  • Move students beyond repeated addition alone so they also interpret rows, columns, and equal groups.
  • Press for the meaning of each factor; the numbers in the equation should stay connected to the model.
  • Use more than one representation for the same situation so students see the same idea across models.
  • Ask students to explain how the model proves the equation, not just how to compute.

Priority mathematical practices

Use these practices as the most natural places to strengthen student thinking while teaching this standard.

  • MP2Reason abstractly and quantitatively: Help students connect groups, arrays, repeated addition, and equations so the factors and product keep their meaning.
  • MP4Model with mathematics: Use visual and contextual models that make the multiplication or division structure visible.
